OVERVIEW
Whitepaper by effectiveness expert Peter Field analysing a decade of IPA (Institute of Practitioners in Advertising) Effectiveness Databank entries. The paper demonstrates TV's sustained and growing role in advertising effectiveness, drawing on 10 years of hard evidence from IPA award entries.

KEY CONCLUSION
Field's analysis of 10 years of effectiveness data suggests that "TV's relationship with effectiveness is growing." TV remains "remarkably effective" as an advertising medium despite fragmentation of media consumption.

THREE FACTORS EXPLAINING TV'S EFFECTIVENESS

1. Attention
TV commands higher levels of genuine viewer attention than other media. The research examines both volume of attention (time spent watching) and quality of attention (engagement and processing depth). TV advertising benefits from contextual attention — viewers are in a receptive, lean-back mindset compared to scrolling or active task-completion contexts.

2. Emotional Clout
TV's audio-visual format enables emotional storytelling at a scale other channels cannot match. Emotional advertising is demonstrably more effective at driving long-term brand growth and market share gains. IPA data consistently shows that emotionally-led campaigns, which TV is particularly suited to deliver, generate stronger business effects than rational/informational approaches. Campaigns optimising for emotional impact outperform those optimising for immediate response.

3. Trust
TV advertising commands higher trust than other media. The public, broadcast nature of TV advertising — its visibility and perceived cost — generates a halo of credibility. Brands that advertise on TV are perceived as more trustworthy, financially stable, and committed to their customers. This trust effect compounds over time, building mental availability and brand salience that drives sales even when consumers are not actively considering a purchase.

IPA DATA EVIDENCE
The IPA Effectiveness Databank is the world's largest repository of evidence-based marketing effectiveness case studies. Peter Field's analysis of entries over a decade shows:
- TV-led campaigns generate the broadest and deepest business effects
- The combination of TV's reach with emotional storytelling creates disproportionate long-term brand value
- Brands reducing TV investment tend to see declining mental availability and market share over time
- TV is particularly effective for driving "very large" business effects — the headline business outcomes that transform category positions

PLANNING IMPLICATIONS
TV should be considered not merely as one channel among many but as the foundation of an effective media strategy, particularly for brands seeking long-term growth. The paper supports integrating TV with digital channels where TV builds the brand platform that makes all other channels more effective — a "multiplier" effect on the total media plan.

The evidence suggests that brands which maintain or increase TV investment through economic uncertainty consistently outperform those that retreat to performance-only channels.
